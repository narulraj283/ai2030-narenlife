    COCA-COLA: STRATEGIC PORTFOLIO TRANSFORMATION IN MATURE BEVERAGE MARKETS

    A Macro Intelligence Memo | June 2030 | CEO Edition

    From: The 2030 Report
    Date: June 2030
    Re: Coca-Cola's Strategy for Generating Investor Returns in Declining Carbonated Beverage Markets (2024-2030)

    EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

    The Chief Executive Officer of The Coca-Cola Company between 2024 and 2030 navigated a fundamental structural challenge in the global beverage industry: carbonated soft drinks, which had generated the vast majority of Coca-Cola's profits for 130 years, faced secular decline in developed markets due to health consciousness, shifting consumer preferences, and regulatory pressures (sugar taxes, portion restrictions). The company's traditional business model‚Äîgenerating approximately 58 percent of revenues from carbonated soft drinks (Coca-Cola, Sprite, Fanta, and variants)‚Äîwas increasingly constrained. Carbonated beverage volumes in developed markets (North America, Europe) declined approximately 1.8-2.2 percent annually between 2024 and June 2030 as consumers shifted toward healthier alternatives. To offset this decline and meet shareholder expectations for 4-5 percent revenue growth, the CEO executed a comprehensive portfolio transformation strategy: (1) acquired leading non-carbonated brands (juice, water, sports drinks) to diversify revenue base, (2) implemented aggressive pricing increases in emerging markets (India, Brazil, Southeast Asia) where price elasticity was lower and volumes were still growing, and (3) targeted $1.5 billion in annual cost reductions by 2033 to offset volume declines with margin expansion. By June 2030, this strategy was executing successfully: non-carbonated segments had grown from 40 percent of revenue (2024) to 42.1 percent of revenue (June 2030), emerging market revenues had grown 8.2 percent annually (versus 1.1 percent in developed markets), and cost reduction initiatives had generated $680 million in annual savings. The company achieved 4.3 percent revenue growth over six years (2024-June 2030)‚Äîslightly above historical average and sufficient to maintain shareholder support. However, the CEO's strategy came at cost of volume declines in the core carbonated beverage business and aggressive pricing in emerging markets that risked triggering volume declines if sustained. The strategic challenge looking toward 2035 was whether the portfolio transformation toward non-carbonated beverages could accelerate quickly enough to offset continued carbonated beverage decline, or whether Coca-Cola would eventually face recognition that the global beverage market was slower-growing than historical investors had assumed.

    SUMMARY: THE BEAR CASE vs. THE BULL CASE

    THE BEAR CASE (Base Case: Gradual Portfolio Shift, Margin Pressure)
    Conservative portfolio transition. By June 2030: Revenue $50B (+3% growth), operating margin 23.5%, stock $65, market cap $380B. Carbonated beverage decline offset by non-carbonated growth but margins compress from pricing power loss.

    THE BULL CASE (Aggressive 2025 CEO Action: Premium Mix Shift + AI Cost Reduction)
    Aggressive portfolio transformation toward premium non-carbonated brands + aggressive manufacturing automation + significant M&A for high-margin juice/sports brands:
    - 2025 capex: $3-4B for manufacturing automation, premium brand acquisitions
    - 2030 revenue: $52B (+4% growth, vs. $50B base), non-carbonated 48% of mix
    - Operating margin: 26.5% (vs. 23.5% base, +300 bps)
    - Stock price: $85 (+30% vs. base)
    - Market cap: $500B (+31%)

    Bull case premium mix shift + cost automation drives margin expansion and multiple re-rating (+31% valuation).

    SECTION ONE: COCA-COLA'S MARKET POSITION AND BUSINESS MODEL (2024)

    The Coca-Cola Company, incorporated in 1886 and headquartered in Atlanta, Georgia, operates globally as the world's largest beverage company by volume and market capitalization. The company does not manufacture beverages directly; rather, it licenses brands to regional and global bottling partners who manufacture and distribute products. This asset-light model generated enormous profitability from brand licensing, concentrate manufacturing, and corporate functions.

    Coca-Cola's revenue and product structure in 2024:

    Carbonated Soft Drinks (58.2% of revenue): Coca-Cola Classic, Diet Coke, Coca-Cola Zero, Sprite, Fanta, Dasani sparkling water, and regional variants. Generated approximately $32.2 billion in annual revenue and approximately 68 percent of operating profit due to high margins and global scale.

    Water and Enhanced Water (14.3% of revenue): Smartwater, Dasani, Minewata (premium brands), and local water brands. Generated approximately $7.9 billion in annual revenue and approximately 12 percent of operating profit. Water was fast-growing segment with attractive margins.

    Juice and Juice Drinks (13.1% of revenue): Minute Maid, Simply Orange, Fanta Juice, and regional brands. Generated approximately $7.3 billion in annual revenue and approximately 10 percent of operating profit. Growth was modest due to declining juice consumption in developed markets.

    Sports Drinks and Performance Beverages (8.4% of revenue): Powerade, Gatorade (minority stake through partnership), and emerging brands. Generated approximately $4.6 billion in annual revenue and approximately 7 percent of operating profit. Growth was moderate but consistent.

    Other Beverages (6.0% of revenue): Tea, coffee, emerging categories. Generated approximately $3.3 billion in annual revenue and approximately 3 percent of operating profit.

    Total revenue in 2024 was approximately $55.3 billion. Operating margins exceeded 32 percent, generating operating profit of approximately $17.7 billion.

    SECTION TWO: THE STRUCTURAL CHALLENGE‚ÄîDECLINING CARBONATED CONSUMPTION

    The fundamental challenge facing the CEO was structural decline in carbonated soft drink consumption in developed markets. Multiple long-term trends were compressing carbonated beverage demand:

    Health and Wellness Trends: Consumers increasingly perceived carbonated soft drinks, particularly sugar-sweetened versions, as unhealthy. Consumption of sugar-sweetened beverages was associated with obesity, diabetes, and dental health deterioration. These health concerns were backed by substantial scientific research and public health messaging. As a result, consumers were shifting toward perceived healthier alternatives: still water, herbal teas, plant-based beverages, and low-sugar/zero-sugar alternatives.

    Regulatory Pressures: Governments globally implemented taxes on sugar-sweetened beverages (UK, France, Mexico, and many others). These taxes increased retail prices of carbonated soft drinks 10-20 percent, pricing the products upward in the price-sensitive segments. Additionally, regulations on portion sizes, school availability, and marketing were restricting market access.

    Demographic Shifts: Younger consumers (millennials and Gen Z) had lower carbonated beverage consumption than prior generations. Carbonated soft drink consumption among consumers aged 13-24 was approximately 30 percent lower than consumption among consumers aged 45-64. This demographic shift meant that as older consumers aged out and younger consumers maintained lower consumption, carbonated beverage volumes would continue to decline.

    The empirical data was clear: carbonated soft drink volumes in developed markets (North America, Europe, developed Asia) had declined approximately 2.1 percent annually between 2014-2024 and continued declining approximately 1.8-2.2 percent annually between 2024-2030. Against volumes declining approximately 1.9 percent annually, Coca-Cola was achieving 4.3 percent revenue growth, indicating the company was compensating volume declines through pricing and portfolio shift.

    SECTION THREE: STRATEGIC RESPONSE‚ÄîPORTFOLIO DIVERSIFICATION

    The CEO's strategic response was comprehensive portfolio transformation away from dependence on carbonated beverages toward growing non-carbonated segments. This strategy involved multiple components:

    First: Accelerated Acquisitions in Non-Carbonated Categories (2024-2030)

    The company completed several major acquisitions:
    - Acquired Bodyarmor (sports drink brand) from Keurig Dr Pepper for approximately $5.8 billion in 2024, immediately establishing presence in the fast-growing sports drink category
    - Acquired fairlife (premium milk and plant-based beverage brand) from dairy company for approximately $1.8 billion in 2026
    - Acquired Control (premium water brand) from European distributor for approximately $1.2 billion in 2028
    - Expanded stake in various emerging non-carbonated brands in emerging markets

    These acquisitions cost approximately $8.8 billion in capital but provided access to high-growth categories with more favorable long-term trajectories than carbonated beverages.

    Second: Portfolio Rebalancing Target

    The CEO established explicit target: non-carbonated beverages should represent 45 percent of revenue by 2035 (from 41.8 percent in June 2030, up from 40 percent in 2024). This rebalancing would require continued acquisitions and organic growth in non-carbonated segments combined with accepting (or actively managing) carbonated beverage decline.

    The rebalancing strategy acknowledged fundamental market reality: the global beverage industry was shifting away from carbonated beverages toward water, juices, sports drinks, and emerging categories. Coca-Cola's participation in these segments would determine whether the company remained the global beverage market leader into the 2030s-2040s.

    Third: Emerging Market Pricing Discipline

    Concurrent with portfolio diversification in developed markets, the CEO implemented aggressive pricing increases in emerging markets where consumption was still growing and where price elasticity was lower than in developed markets. In markets like India, Brazil, Southeast Asia, and Mexico, per-capita beverage consumption was significantly lower than in developed markets, creating volume growth opportunity if prices remained accessible. However, where volume growth existed, management implemented pricing increases of approximately 5-8 percent annually, exceeding cost inflation of 2-3 percent.

    The pricing strategy balanced growth and margin: modest price increases (5-8 percent) in emerging markets maintained volume growth while generating margin expansion. In contrast, developed market pricing was more modest (2-4 percent annually) to limit volume declines.

    SECTION FOUR: FINANCIAL PERFORMANCE AND EXECUTION (2024-2030)

    Coca-Cola's financial execution of this strategy was successful:

    Revenue Evolution:
    - 2024: $55.3 billion
    - 2025: $57.1 billion (+3.2%)
    - 2026: $58.9 billion (+3.2%)
    - 2027: $60.8 billion (+3.2%)
    - 2028: $62.2 billion (+2.3%)
    - 2029: $63.8 billion (+2.6%)
    - June 2030: $64.6 billion (annualized) (+3.1% from 2024, CAGR 4.3% if annualized to end-2030)

    Revenue growth of approximately 4.3 percent CAGR was achieved through combination of pricing (approximately 2.8 percent average annual increase), non-carbonated growth (approximately 7-9 percent annually), emerging market growth (approximately 8.2 percent annually), and carbonated beverage decline (approximately -2.1 percent annually).

    Volume Growth Evolution:
    - 2024: Approximately 1.9 billion unit cases of beverages sold globally
    - June 2030: Approximately 1.88 billion unit cases (annualized) (-0.6% CAGR)

    Despite revenue growth, volume was essentially flat to slightly declining, indicating price increases were the primary driver of revenue growth.

    Operating Profit and Margin Evolution:
    - 2024: $17.7 billion (32.0% margin)
    - 2025: $18.2 billion (31.9% margin)
    - 2026: $18.9 billion (32.1% margin)
    - 2027: $19.6 billion (32.2% margin)
    - 2028: $20.1 billion (32.3% margin)
    - June 2030: $20.8 billion (annualized, 32.2% margin)

    Operating margins remained stable at approximately 32 percent, indicating the company successfully offset volume declines through pricing and cost management.

    Cash Generation and Dividend Performance:
    - 2024: Operating cash flow approximately $11.2 billion; Dividends $6.8 billion
    - June 2030: Operating cash flow approximately $11.8 billion (annualized); Dividends $7.4 billion

    Dividend growth from $6.8 billion to $7.4 billion annually (approximately 2.0 percent CAGR) was modest. The company prioritized maintenance of dividend yield (approximately 3.1 percent at June 2030) over aggressive dividend growth.

    Stock Price Evolution:
    - June 2024: $60.80
    - June 2027: $66.90
    - June 2030: $71.40

    Stock price appreciation of 17.4 percent over six years represented approximately 2.7 percent annualized return. Combined with dividends, total shareholder return was approximately 20-21 percent over six years, or approximately 3.0-3.2 percent annualized‚Äîbelow historical equity returns of 8-10 percent but appropriate for a mature, low-growth business in declining market.

    SECTION FIVE: MARKET SHARE AND COMPETITIVE POSITION

    Coca-Cola maintained global beverage market leadership despite the company's facing transition challenges:

    • Global beverage market share: Approximately 48.2 percent (June 2030), stable from 48.4 percent in 2024
    • In carbonated beverages specifically: Approximately 44.1 percent market share (declining from 45.8 percent in 2024 as PepsiCo and other competitors gained share)
    • In emerging markets: Approximately 52.3 percent market share (growing from 50.1 percent in 2024)
    • In developed markets: Approximately 44.8 percent market share (declining from 46.2 percent in 2024)

    The market share dynamics reflected the CEO's strategic positioning: accepting share loss in declining carbonated beverage category while focusing on emerging markets and non-carbonated growth. This was rational strategic choice given market fundamentals.

    SECTION SIX: COST REDUCTION INITIATIVES AND EFFICIENCY

    To offset carbonated beverage volume declines and margin pressures, the CEO initiated comprehensive cost reduction program targeting $1.5 billion in annual cost savings by 2033. Progress through June 2030:

    • Annual cost reductions achieved: Approximately $680 million
    • Primary cost reduction areas:
    • Supply chain optimization and reduction of redundant distribution centers: approximately $210 million annually
    • Manufacturing efficiency improvements through automation and AI-powered optimization: approximately $180 million annually
    • Corporate function consolidation and back-office automation: approximately $150 million annually
    • Marketing efficiency improvements through digital channels and AI-optimized media buying: approximately $140 million annually

    Cost reduction initiatives were on track to achieve $1.5 billion target by 2033 if trajectory continued.

    SECTION SEVEN: FORWARD CHALLENGES AND STRATEGY VIABILITY

    By June 2030, several challenges threatened the sustainability of the CEO's strategy:

    First: Emerging Market Pricing Sustainability:
    Aggressive pricing in emerging markets (5-8 percent annually) risked triggering volume declines if sustained. Already, volume growth in emerging markets was moderating: India soft drink volumes had declined from 2.8 percent growth (2025-2027) to 1.2 percent growth (2028-2030) due to cumulative pricing. If emerging market pricing continued at 6 percent annually while inflation was only 3-4 percent, the price-consumption trade-off would eventually cause volume declines.

    Second: Non-Carbonated Acquisition Integration:
    The company's strategy depended on acquiring and integrating leading non-carbonated brands (Bodyarmor, fairlife, Control). However, integration of acquired brands into the Coca-Cola system required maintaining founder vision and brand independence while capturing operational synergies. Acquisition integrations had mixed success; some acquisitions (Bodyarmor) had thrived while others required significant operational restructuring.

    Third: Long-Term Market Growth:
    Global beverage consumption growth was fundamentally constrained. Total global beverage consumption was approximately 1.9 billion unit cases in 2030, growing only 0.6 percent annually. This slow growth, combined with Coca-Cola's need to demonstrate investor returns, meant the company would need to gain share or implement pricing discipline to deliver results. Neither path was unlimited.

    CONCLUSION

    The CEO of Coca-Cola between 2024 and 2030 navigated the fundamental strategic challenge of transforming a company built on declining business (carbonated soft drinks) toward more favorable growth categories (non-carbonated beverages, emerging markets). Through portfolio acquisition, pricing discipline, and cost management, the CEO delivered approximately 4.3 percent revenue growth and maintained operating margins and dividend stability. However, the CEO's strategy‚Äîaccepting carbonated beverage decline while pursuing non-carbonated growth and emerging market pricing‚Äîcontained inherent tensions that would likely intensify by 2035. The forward challenge was whether portfolio transformation toward non-carbonated beverages could accelerate quickly enough to maintain 4+ percent growth rates, or whether Coca-Cola would eventually need to acknowledge that the global beverage market was fundamentally slower-growing than historical investors had expected, requiring adjusted return expectations.
