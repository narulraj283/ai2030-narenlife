    THE INFLECTION: 2029

    2029 revealed two unexpected problems:

    Problem 1: On-device AI is genuinely slower and less capable than cloud AI.

    Despite Apple's engineering excellence, on-device models can't match the capability of cloud-hosted LLMs. An iPhone image generation model is slower and produces lower-quality images than DALL-E or Midjourney. Writing assistance is good, but not as good as ChatGPT.

    Apple's customers noticed. More importantly, Apple's developers noticed. And when Apple Intelligence features weren't competitive, the value proposition for upgrading from iPhone 14 or 15 to iPhone 16/17 diminished.

    Problem 2: AI agents make apps less necessary.

    This was the deeper disruption. As AI agents (particularly ChatGPT, Gemini) became more capable at understanding user intent and taking actions, the "app" as a unit of consumption became less relevant.

    Consider: A user wants to book a flight. Historically, they opened the Skyscanner app, searched, found a flight, booked it. Apple captured a 15-30% commission on that transaction through the App Store.

    By 2029, the user asks ChatGPT "find me the cheapest flight to London next week." ChatGPT (or Gemini) searches, compares airlines, finds the cheapest option, and shows the user the booking link. The user taps the link and books directly with the airline. Apple never touched the transaction.

    This pattern repeated across thousands of apps. AI agents disintermediated apps.

    - Users don't need multiple Apple devices if AI agent can manage all tasks

    - Cross-device syncing becomes less valuable if cloud AI agent handles tasks

    Lock-In Strength:
    - iMessage remains tightly integrated with Apple ecosystem (35-40% of ecosystem value)
    - Health data integration with wearables creates stickiness (20-25% of ecosystem value)

    Net Assessment: Lock-in weakening as AI agents disintermediate, but stickiness remains on health/wearables and messaging (30-40% of ecosystem value remains sticky).
