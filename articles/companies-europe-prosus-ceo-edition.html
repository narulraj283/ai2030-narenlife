    PROSUS: VENTURE CAPITAL PORTFOLIO AT PUBLIC COMPANY SCALE

    A Macro Intelligence Memo | June 2030 | CEO Edition

    From: The 2030 Report
    Date: June 2030
    Re: Leadership Challenges in Managing Venture Capital Portfolio as Public Company

    SUMMARY: THE BEAR CASE vs. THE BULL CASE

    BEAR CASE (Portfolio Monetization Focus - Actual Path)

    Prosus divests legacy assets (Classifieds, some Asian holdings), focuses on fintech and e-commerce, achieves 8-10% NAV growth annually. Tencent holdings remain ~25% of NAV. Operating investments reach ‚Ç¨3-4B EBITDA. Return on capital: 12-14%. Dividend ‚Ç¨0.25-0.35 per share. Stock appreciation targets 8-10% annually.

    Financial Impact (Bear Case 2035):
    - NAV per Share: $105-125
    - Investment Portfolio Value: $85-100B
    - Operating Earnings (non-Tencent): ‚Ç¨4-5B
    - Stock CAGR 2030-2035: 8-10%

    BULL CASE (Aggressive Fintech Investment - 2025 Commitment)

    Had Prosus committed ‚Ç¨10-12B to fintech expansion (payments, lending, insurance) in emerging markets, building 5-6 dominant platforms by 2030, the company would have achieved 14-16% NAV growth and 16-18% returns on capital. Tencent holdings valued at $35-40B. Operating investments generate ‚Ç¨6-8B EBITDA. NAV reaches $150-170 per share by 2030. Stock CAGR reaches 12-14%.

    Executive Summary

    The Chief Executive Officer of Prosus between 2024-2030 navigated one of the most challenging mandates in global corporate leadership: operating a venture capital portfolio (holding stakes in dozens of companies across multiple geographies and sectors) as a publicly traded company subject to quarterly earnings pressure and stock market valuation discipline. Prosus operated fundamentally as a holding company for venture investments (Delivery Hero, Stack Overflow, and 100+ other portfolio companies in various stages) rather than as a traditional operating company. This structure created chronic tension between venture capital logic (patient capital, long-term value creation, tolerance for failure) and public company logic (quarterly results, transparent financial performance, stock price accountability). The CEO made investment decisions, managed portfolio company performance, and navigated investor expectations and NAV (Net Asset Value) discount that plagued the business model. By June 2030, the CEO's legacy was adequate navigation of extreme structural tension without achieving breakthrough success or catastrophic failure‚Äîa respectable but not impressive outcome given the challenging circumstances.

    Section 1: Prosus Business Model and Structural Challenge (2024)

    Prosus as Venture Capital Portfolio Company

    Prosus (and subsidiary Naspers) was fundamentally a venture capital portfolio company publicly traded:

    2024 Prosus Portfolio Overview:
    - Number of portfolio companies: 140+
    - Geographic presence: 60+ countries
    - Major holdings: Delivery Hero (22% stake, ~USD 4B valuation), Stack Overflow (majority), Brainly (majority), various minority stakes
    - Assets under management (portfolio value): ~USD 110 billion (estimated)
    - Public market capitalization: ~USD 74 billion (June 2024)
    - NAV Discount: 33% (portfolio value USD 110B vs. market cap USD 74B)

    The substantial NAV discount reflected investor skepticism about:
    1. Value destruction through management (corporate overhead, inefficient capital allocation)
    2. Tax inefficiency (holding structure created tax drag)
    3. Execution risk (portfolio companies might underperform)
    4. Illiquidity risk (large portfolio stakes were illiquid)

    The Fundamental Tension

    Prosus faced structural tension between venture capital portfolio management and public company discipline:

    Venture Capital Logic:
    - Long-term value creation (5-10 year horizons)
    - Tolerance for portfolio company losses (Delivery Hero losing money for years was acceptable)
    - Concentration in winners (a few huge wins drive returns)
    - Patient capital deployment

    Public Company Logic:
    - Quarterly financial reporting
    - Stock price accountability
    - Transparent financial performance
    - Dividend expectations (if profitable)
    - Institutional investor pressure for consistent results

    These logics were often in conflict. Investors wanted Prosus to be a venture capital fund (patient, long-term, accepting of losses), but Prosus was structured as public company requiring quarterly results.

    Section 2: Portfolio Performance and Valuation Challenges (2024-2030)

    Portfolio Company Performance Heterogeneity

    Prosus's portfolio companies experienced wildly divergent trajectories:

    Major Portfolio Companies Performance (2024-2030):

    Winners:
    - Stack Overflow (developer Q&A platform): Increased valuation from USD 1.8B (2024) to USD 3.2B (June 2030), +78%
    - Brainly (student knowledge platform): Increased valuation from USD 1.2B (2024) to USD 2.1B (June 2030), +75%
    - iFood (Brazilian food delivery): Increased valuation from USD 3.2B (2024) to USD 5.8B (June 2030), +81%

    Losers/Challenged:
    - Delivery Hero (global food delivery): Increased valuation from USD 18B (2024) to USD 22B (June 2030), +22%, but still unprofitable after years of investment
    - PayTM (Indian payments): Declined valuation from USD 16B (2024) to USD 11B (June 2030), -31%
    - Other early-stage companies: Mixed results, many remaining unprofitable

    Net Portfolio Impact:
    - Portfolio value growth 2024-2030: +18% nominal (USD 110B ‚Üí USD 130B)
    - Annualized return: ~2.8%
    - Significantly underperformance vs. public equity markets (+28% CAGR S&P 500)
    - Significant underperformance vs. venture capital private market benchmarks (+35%+ typical)

    The portfolio achieved modest returns that underperformed both public equity and venture capital benchmarks.

    The Delivery Hero Challenge

    The largest source of portfolio tension was Delivery Hero, Prosus's flagship holding (22% stake, USD 4-5B valuation):

    Delivery Hero Performance (2024-2030):
    - Revenue growth: Strong, consistent 25%+ annual growth
    - Path to profitability: Repeatedly delayed
    - Annual losses: USD 600M-1B annually through 2030
    - Stock performance: Underperformance vs. peers
    - CEO/Board changes: Multiple leadership transitions

    Investor Frustration:
    Prosus had invested USD 8-10B cumulatively in Delivery Hero via direct investment and Naspers parent company. The company remained deeply unprofitable despite years of capital deployment, creating investor skepticism about management's capital discipline.

    The CEO's Challenge:
    As Prosus CEO, had to maintain support for Delivery Hero (largest investment, strategic importance) while responding to investor criticism about value destruction. Difficult balance: support management to fix problems vs. pressure management to achieve profitability.

    NAV Discount Persistence

    The NAV discount (33% in 2024) persisted throughout 2024-2030:

    NAV Discount Evolution:
    - 2024: 33% (portfolio USD 110B vs. market cap USD 74B)
    - 2025: 35% (portfolio USD 115B vs. market cap USD 75B)
    - 2026: 32% (portfolio USD 125B vs. market cap USD 85B)
    - 2027: 34% (portfolio USD 128B vs. market cap USD 84B)
    - 2028: 33% (portfolio USD 133B vs. market cap USD 89B)
    - 2029: 35% (portfolio USD 136B vs. market cap USD 88B)
    - June 2030: 33% (portfolio USD 130B vs. market cap USD 87B)

    Interpretation:
    The persistent 30-35% NAV discount reflected investor skepticism that was never resolved. Prosus's public shares traded at 65-70 cents on the dollar of net asset value throughout the period, suggesting:
    - Investors valued portfolio companies at lower prices than management's estimates
    - Investors discounted future value creation by management
    - Structural inefficiency (corporate overhead, taxes) reduced value by 30-35%

    The CEO could not close this discount despite adequate operational management.

    Section 3: Investment Decision-Making and Capital Deployment

    Capital Deployment and Portfolio Rebalancing

    The CEO made several investment decisions during 2024-2030:

    Major Investment/Divestment Decisions:

    1. Increased Delivery Hero Commitment (2024-2026):
    - Provided additional capital to Delivery Hero for market consolidation
    - Defended stake against investor criticism
    - Rationale: Delivery Hero had largest winning potential; needed capital to consolidate market

    2. Stack Overflow Acquisition (2024):
    - Acquired remaining stake in Stack Overflow for USD 1.8B
    - Developer Q&A platform with strong network effects, attractive demographics
    - Rationale: High-potential acquisition at reasonable price

    3. Geographic Expansion in Emerging Markets (2025-2026):
    - Increased stakes in Southeast Asian and Latin American companies
    - Deployed USD 2-3B in new market expansion
    - Rationale: Growth potential in emerging markets

    4. European/US Venture Portfolio Building (2027-2028):
    - Built positions in US and European venture companies
    - Reduced concentrated emerging market exposure
    - Rationale: Diversification, risk management

    5. Selective Exits (2028-2029):
    - Divested stakes in underperforming companies
    - Realized losses on some investments
    - Deployed proceeds into better-performing assets

    Overall Capital Deployment (2024-2030):
    - New capital deployed into portfolio: USD 12-15B
    - Exits and reductions: USD 8-10B
    - Net new capital: USD 4-5B

    Investment Returns and Allocation Decisions

    The CEO's investment decisions delivered mixed results:

    Performance Attribution:
    - Geographic diversification: Positive; emerging markets outperformed expectations
    - Stack Overflow acquisition: Positive; company exceeded growth targets
    - Delivery Hero support: Mixed; company remained unprofitable but market share grew
    - New venture investments: Mixed; some succeeded, many remained early-stage

    Investor Assessment of CEO's Decisions:
    - Generally considered competent but not exceptional
    - Some decisions questioned (e.g., continued Delivery Hero support)
    - Others praised (e.g., Stack Overflow, geographic diversification)
    - Overall: Adequate portfolio management without breakthrough decisions

    Section 4: Quarterly Financial Performance and Investor Communication

    Earnings and Financial Results

    Prosus's financial results reflected portfolio company performance:

    Prosus Consolidated Financial Results (2024-2030):

    Year Portfolio Value Investment Gains/Losses Operating Profit Net Profit EPS
    2024 110B +2.1B 1.2B 3.3B 1.05
    2025 115B -0.8B 1.4B 0.6B 0.19
    2026 125B +4.2B 1.6B 5.8B 1.84
    2027 128B +1.5B 1.5B 3.0B 0.95

    2029 136B -0.4B 1.8B 1.4B 0.44
    June 2030 130B -1.2B 1.9B 0.7B 0.22

    Key observations:

    1. Earnings Volatility:
    EPS ranged from 0.19 (2025) to 1.84 (2026) driven by portfolio valuation swings. For public company investors expecting stability, this volatility was problematic.

    2. Investment Gains Volatility:
    Net profit was heavily influenced by unrealized gains/losses in portfolio, creating quarter-to-quarter unpredictability.

    3. Operating Profit Stability:
    Underlying operating profit (excluding investment gains) was relatively stable (USD 1.2-1.9B), but was small relative to portfolio value, suggesting low operating leverage.

    Investor Communication Challenges

    The CEO faced persistent investor communication challenges:

    Recurring Investor Questions (2024-2030):
    1. "Why hold the portfolio as public company? Why not split into separate public companies?"
    2. "When will Delivery Hero be profitable?"
    3. "How do you justify the NAV discount?"
    4. "Why not increase dividend payouts if you're running out of growth ideas?"
    5. "Is this the right capital allocation vs. returning capital to shareholders?"

    CEO's Responses:
    1. Argued that portfolio company synergies justified holding structure
    2. Committed to Delivery Hero profitability "within 2-3 years" (recurring answer)
    3. Argued NAV discount reflected investor skepticism but would narrow over time
    4. Maintained modest dividend policy (1-2% yield)
    5. Emphasized long-term value creation logic

    Investor Reception:
    Skeptical. Responses were reasonable but not fully convincing. The structural issues (NAV discount, earnings volatility, Delivery Hero unprofitability) remained unresolved.

    Section 5: Strategic Options and Unresolved Challenges

    The Unresolved Strategic Question

    The CEO faced a fundamental unresolved question: "What is Prosus?"

    Option 1: Venture Capital Fund at Public Market Scale
    - Accept earnings volatility, unrealized gains/losses
    - Focus on long-term portfolio value creation
    - Accept NAV discount as structural feature
    - Investor base: Patient capital, long-term investors

    Option 2: Operating Company Using Venture Capital as Leverage
    - Consolidate portfolio companies toward single coherent business
    - Focus on profitability of consolidated business
    - Reduce complexity and NAV discount
    - Investor base: Public equity investors expecting stability

    Option 3: Portfolio Split (Separate Holdings)
    - Separate portfolio companies into distinct public companies
    - Eliminate NAV discount by eliminating holding company
    - Accept loss of portfolio synergies
    - Investor base: Index funds, sector-focused investors

    By June 2030, the CEO had not decisively chosen any option. Prosus remained a hybrid:
    - More complex than pure operating company
    - More constrained than pure venture capital fund
    - Suffering from structural inefficiencies of both models without benefits of either

    Why the CEO Couldn't Decide

    The strategic choice was complicated because:
    1. Shareholder heterogeneity: Different shareholders preferred different options
    2. Delivery Hero uncertainty: Unclear whether core holding was worth continued investment
    3. Tax considerations: Separating holdings created complex tax consequences
    4. Path dependency: History of investments created sunk cost and emotional attachment
    5. Market timing: Optimal time for separation/restructuring was unclear

    The CEO lacked clear mandate to decisively restructure, and proceeded with incrementalism.

    Section 6: CEO Assessment

    What the CEO Achieved

    The CEO's tenure (2024-2030) was defined by competent management of structural complexity:

    1. Portfolio Navigation:
    - Managed portfolio through volatile period (2024-2030 was global tech volatility)
    - Made reasonable investment decisions (Stack Overflow, geographic diversification)
    - Avoided catastrophic losses while capturing some upside

    2. Shareholder Communication:
    - Maintained investor confidence despite repeated questions and skepticism
    - Provided transparent quarterly reporting
    - Avoided major scandals or controversies

    3. Operational Efficiency:
    - Maintained operating profit stability despite portfolio volatility
    - Managed overhead reasonably effectively

    What the CEO Could Not Achieve

    The CEO's tenure was limited by structural constraints:

    1. NAV Discount Closure:
    The 30-35% NAV discount persisted throughout tenure, reflecting fundamental investor skepticism that operational management could not overcome.

    2. Delivery Hero Resolution:
    Delivery Hero remained unprofitable after years of capital deployment, frustrated investors, but CEO could not force profitability through holding company structure.

    3. Strategic Clarity:
    The fundamental question of "what is Prosus?" remained unresolved. The company remained an awkward hybrid without clear strategic positioning.

    4. Earnings Stability:
    Quarterly volatility remained due to portfolio company valuation swings, making institutional investor holdings unattractive.

    Assessment of Performance

    The CEO's performance was adequate but not impressive:

    • Operational execution: B+ (competent but not exceptional)
    • Strategic vision: C+ (pragmatic incrementalism, not bold decisions)
    • Shareholder value creation: B (adequate returns but below bench marks)
    • Investor satisfaction: C (persistent skepticism about business model)

    For a CEO in an inherently difficult structural situation, the performance was reasonable. However, a more decisive CEO might have made bold choices (portfolio separation, Delivery Hero exit, strategic repositioning) that could have either destroyed or created tremendous value. The actual CEO chose pragmatic incrementalism, avoiding disaster but missing opportunity for transformation.

    Conclusion

    The CEO of Prosus between 2024-2030 navigated a business model fundamentally at odds with public company structure: operating a venture capital portfolio as a publicly traded company. The tension between venture capital logic (patient, long-term, accepting of losses) and public company logic (quarterly results, stock price accountability) was never resolved.

    The CEO's approach was pragmatic incrementalism: make reasonable investment decisions, manage portfolio through volatility, communicate transparently, and avoid major strategic decisions that might disrupt the status quo. This resulted in adequate financial performance and maintained investor confidence, but did not resolve fundamental structural issues (NAV discount, earnings volatility, Delivery Hero unprofitability, strategic ambiguity).

    By June 2030, Prosus remained what it had been in 2024: a large venture portfolio publicly traded at significant discount to net asset value, generating volatile earnings, with unclear strategic direction. The CEO successfully navigated this awkward structure without causing catastrophic damage, but did not transform the company or resolve the strategic questions facing it. For a CEO in such structural constraints, this represents a respectable if unglamorous outcome.
