    EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

    Prosus, the Amsterdam-listed venture capital conglomerate and Naspers subsidiary, has delivered compounded annual returns of 1.2% to shareholders from 2024 through June 2030, underperforming inflation and the broader market. The company's $87.3 billion portfolio‚Äîcentered on a 27.2% stake in Tencent, food delivery operations, classifieds platforms, and emerging market fintech‚Äînavigated extreme valuation cycles driven by AI enthusiasm (2024-2025), profitability reckoning (2025-2028), and portfolio rebalancing (2028-2030).

    The core challenge Prosus has faced is structural: as a public vehicle for private venture capital, it suffers from a persistent 18-24% discount to net asset value, tax inefficiency from concentrated illiquid holdings, and conflicting incentives between the venture capital mandate (high risk, concentrated bets) and public market expectations (diversification, visible cash generation).

    By June 2030, Prosus has initiated a strategic shift toward portfolio simplification and capital optimization, divesting underperforming assets and increasing focus on cash-generative properties. The Tencent stake‚Äîwhich represented 65% of NAV in 2024‚Äîhas declined to 58% of NAV by 2030, reflecting Prosus's deliberate portfolio reweighting.

    SECTION ONE: PORTFOLIO COMPOSITION AND EVOLUTION

    2024 Portfolio Structure

    In January 2024, Prosus's $87.3 billion portfolio comprised:

    Asset Class Value ($B) % of NAV Strategic Significance
    Tencent (27.2% stake) $56.8 65.1% Core holding; Chinese tech/games/fintech
    Delivery Hero $8.2 9.4% Food delivery platform; cash burn concerns
    Food Delivery Ops (Brazil, Poland, etc.) $12.1 13.9% Direct operations; variable profitability
    Classifieds Platforms $5.4 6.2% eMarketplaces (low growth, high margin)
    Fintech Emerging Markets $2.8 3.2% Early-stage bets; mostly pre-revenue
    Cash and Other $2.0 2.2% Dry powder

    The portfolio structure reflected Naspers's historical investment thesis: deploy capital across Asian and emerging market tech platforms, maintain long-term strategic stakes, and harvest gains through IPO or strategic exit.

    The Tencent Anchor

    Tencent represented the crown jewel of the Prosus portfolio. The 27.2% stake was valued at $56.8 billion in January 2024 (based on Tencent's market cap of $208 billion). Tencent's business comprised:
    - Social media and messaging (WeChat): 93% of Chinese internet users
    - Online games: $14 billion in annual revenue; 21% of global gaming market
    - Fintech (WeChat Pay, Tenpay): Processing $2.1 trillion in annual transactions
    - Cloud and enterprise software: Emerging but growing segment

    The Tencent stake was both Prosus's greatest asset and its greatest liability:
    - Asset: Exposure to one of the world's most profitabletech companies, with $40 billion in annual EBITDA
    - Liability: Extreme concentration; inability to diversify without triggering Chinese tax law complications; regulatory uncertainty

    The Delivery Hero Conundrum

    Delivery Hero was Prosus's second-largest holding (9.4% of NAV). Acquired stakes in food delivery platforms across multiple geographies created a global presence:
    - Brazil: 35% market share (competing with Ifood)
    - Central Europe: 42% market share (competing with Wolt)
    - Southeast Asia: 18% market share (competing with Grab, Gojek)

    Delivery Hero's business model was structurally challenged:
    - Unit economics: Food delivery operates at negative unit economics (delivery cost > commission margin)
    - Path to profitability: Unclear; dependent on consolidation, price increases, and scale
    - Cash burn: $800M annually (2024) despite $2.1 billion revenue

    Delivery Hero had been a persistent drag on Prosus returns throughout the 2018-2024 period. By 2024, investor patience was exhausted.

    Prosus's Direct Food Delivery Operations

    Prosus directly operated food delivery platforms in Brazil, Poland, Portugal, and several emerging markets. These operations generated:
    - 2024 revenue: $3.2 billion
    - 2024 operating loss: -$280 million
    - Path to profitability: Contingent on consolidation and unit economics improvement

    These direct operations were structurally similar to Delivery Hero but had even less potential for profitability, given the fragmented competitive landscape.

    Classifieds Platforms

    Prosus owned control stakes in several classifieds platforms:
    - OLX (emerging markets classifieds): $1.8 billion valuation; $180 million revenue; $15 million EBITDA
    - Lokl (Latin American classifieds): $1.2 billion valuation; $85 million revenue; $5 million EBITDA
    - Various others: Combined $2.4 billion valuation

    These were legacy business units from the pre-digital era. While profitable, they faced secular decline as consumers migrated to social media-based marketplaces (Facebook Marketplace, WhatsApp).

    SECTION TWO: VALUATION CYCLES AND STOCK PERFORMANCE (2024-2030)

    2024-2025: AI Enthusiasm and Valuation Expansion

    The period from January 2024 through December 2025 was characterized by explosive enthusiasm for AI-driven software valuations. Tech stocks globally rallied:
    - NASDAQ: +38% (2024-2025)
    - Chinese tech stocks: +22% (despite regulatory concerns)
    - European tech stocks: +31%

    Prosus benefited from two tailwinds:
    1. Tencent valuation expansion: Tencent's market cap expanded from $208 billion to $285 billion (37% increase) as investors re-rated the stock on AI opportunities (gaming AI, WeChat AI assistant capabilities, cloud AI).
    2. Delivery Hero re-rating: Delivery Hero was viewed as a beneficiary of AI-driven logistics optimization. The stock rallied 45% in 2024-2025.

    Prosus stock price appreciated 42% from January 2024 ($18.30/share) to December 2025 ($25.95/share).

    NAV appreciation: NAV increased from $87.3 billion to $119 billion, driven primarily by:
    - Tencent: $56.8B ‚Üí $77.8B (+37%)
    - Delivery Hero: $8.2B ‚Üí $11.9B (+45%)

    However, the NAV discount widened from 21% to 23%, reflecting investor skepticism about the sustainability of growth-stage company valuations.

    2025-2028: Profitability Reckoning and Valuation Reset

    Beginning in Q4 2025, tech investor sentiment shifted dramatically. Multiple pressures converged:

    1. Interest rate inflection (2025-2026): Federal Reserve maintained elevated rates through 2025-2026 to combat persistent inflation. Higher discount rates made unprofitable growth companies significantly less attractive.

    2. Profitability focus: Investors shifted from "growth at all costs" to "profitable growth." Companies burning massive cash (Delivery Hero, DoorDash, Uber Eats) faced intense scrutiny.

    3. Chinese regulatory uncertainty: In Q2 2026, the Chinese government announced new restrictions on gaming time and online content, creating uncertainty about Tencent's long-term growth.

    4. Macro slowdown: European and U.S. economic growth decelerated in 2027, creating headwinds for consumer-facing tech.

    The result was a severe re-rating:

    - Tencent: $77.8B ‚Üí $48.2B (-38%, multiple compression from 3.8x to 2.8x)

    Beginning in Q4 2028, Prosus initiated strategic portfolio rebalancing:

    2. Delivery Hero: Divested 40% of stake in Q1 2029, realizing $1.8 billion

    Tax and Liquidity Constraints

    Prosus's ability to monetize the Tencent stake is constrained by:
    1. Chinese outbound investment regulations: Moving capital out of China is restricted
    2. Tax efficiency: A complete exit would trigger significant tax liability (estimated $15-20 billion)
    3. Shareholder concentration: Naspers controls 50.1% of Prosus; strategic incentives may differ from minority shareholders

    As a result, Prosus is effectively a "long-term holder" of Tencent, unable to dynamically rebalance.

    SECTION FOUR: FOOD DELIVERY: THE BURNING CASH MACHINE

    The Unit Economics Problem

    Food delivery faced inherent unit economics challenges:

    Cost Component % of Order Value
    Delivery cost 28%
    Restaurant commission 8%
    Processing & payment 4%
    Marketing/customer acquisition 6%
    Corporate overhead 5%
    Total costs 51%
    Net margin (before capex) -51%

    At scale, food delivery players are structurally unprofitable at the unit level unless:
    1. Delivery costs decline (through automation: drones, autonomous vehicles)
    2. Commission increases (raising restaurant prices, reducing competitiveness)
    3. Marketing efficiency improves (reducing CAC through network effects)

    Delivery Hero had attempted all three strategies with limited success:

    Strategy 1: Automation
    - Drone delivery pilots in Europe (2025-2026): Limited due to regulatory barriers
    - Autonomous vehicle integration (2027-2028): Minimal penetration (<2% of deliveries)

    Strategy 2: Commission increases
    - Commission raised from 25% to 30% (2026)
    - Result: Restaurants defected to competitors (DoorDash, Gojek)
    - Outcome: Failed strategy

    Strategy 3: Network effects
    - Market consolidation in Brazil (merged with iFood-adjacent competitor): Reduced competition, stabilized commissions
    - Result in other markets: Continued fragmentation

    Prosus's response:

    Prosus's Direct Food Delivery Operations

    Prosus directly operated food delivery platforms in Brazil, Poland, Portugal, and several other markets. These operations faced similar unit economics challenges:

    • 2024 revenue: $3.2 billion
    • 2024 operating loss: -$280 million
    • 2030 revenue: $3.1 billion (flat)

    Management consolidated operations and reduced customer acquisition spending, trending toward breakeven by 2031-2032. However, the secular challenge remains: food delivery is structurally unprofitable absent technological breakthroughs (autonomous delivery).
