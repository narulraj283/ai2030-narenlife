    ENTITY: DELL TECHNOLOGIES

    MACRO INTELLIGENCE MEMO

    From: The 2030 Report
    Date: June 2030
    Re: Dell Technologies - The "Picks and Shovels" Story of Artificial Intelligence Infrastructure

    EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

    Dell Technologies by June 2030 had successfully repositioned itself as the dominant supplier of server infrastructure for artificial intelligence training and inference workloads. The company's fiscal year 2030 financial results reflected this transformation: $103.2 billion in consolidated revenue, $8.7 billion in net income, with the Infrastructure Solutions Group generating $54.2 billion in revenue‚Äîa 118% increase from FY2025 levels.

    This growth trajectory was achieved despite a precipitous 31% decline in the legacy PC business, which contracted from $45.6 billion (FY2025) to $31.4 billion (FY2030) as artificial intelligence agents and cloud computing reduced demand for individual workstations.

    Dell's strategic repositioning as an AI infrastructure provider has vindicated the classical "picks and shovels" investment thesis: during periods of rapid technological transformation, suppliers of foundational infrastructure tools often outperform both the transformative technology itself and the end-use applications built on that technology.

    By June 2030, Dell commanded approximately 52% market share in the AI server infrastructure market, positioning the company as the second-largest infrastructure supplier globally (behind NVIDIA in GPU accelerators) and ahead of traditional competitors like Hewlett Packard Enterprise and Super Micro Computer.

    The company trades at 18.4x forward earnings with a 4.2% free cash flow yield, reflecting valuation that prices in continued AI infrastructure demand growth while acknowledging execution risks and competitive vulnerabilities.

    SUMMARY: THE BEAR CASE vs. THE BULL CASE

    THE BEAR CASE: Hyperscalers accelerate custom chip strategies; Google, Amazon, Meta reduce Dell server purchases 25-35% by 2035 as proprietary silicon dominates data center footprint. AI infrastructure growth moderates to 6-8% annually as compute scaling plateau approaches. Margin compression to 14-15% on competitive intensity and commoditization. Dell's ISG revenue peaks at $65-70B by 2035 (vs. $75-80B base case). PC business continues secular decline below $25B. Combined ISG margin compression and lower growth yields fair valuation of $120-160 (-15% downside), implying -3% to +2% annual returns through 2035.

    THE BULL CASE: Hyperscalers find custom chip development economically inefficient; continue relying on Dell systems integration and domain expertise for 60%+ of infrastructure. AI clusters proliferate beyond projections (reaching 35,000+ by 2035); ISG revenue reaches $90B+ with gross margin expanding to 19-20% through manufacturing scale and product mix. PC business stabilizes at $25-28B with high margins. Dell achieves consolidated $140-145B revenue and $14-16B net income. Stock reaches $280-350 by 2035 (18-25% CAGR returns) driven by growth and operational leverage.

    SECTION 1: THE AI INFRASTRUCTURE TRANSFORMATION

    The Nature of AI Infrastructure Demand

    The explosive growth in large language models and multimodal AI systems between 2024 and 2030 created unprecedented demand for specialized computing infrastructure. This demand operates at multiple scales:

    Large-scale training clusters are utilized by major technology companies (OpenAI, Google DeepMind, Meta, Anthropic) and increasingly by enterprise software companies to train proprietary large language models. A typical large-scale training cluster (1,000+ GPU-equivalents) requires:

    • 500-750 specialized servers configured for GPU/accelerator operation
    • Sophisticated networking infrastructure (high-bandwidth, low-latency interconnects)
    • Storage systems for training datasets and model checkpoints
    • Total infrastructure investment ranging from $4-6 billion for a single large cluster
    • Annual operational costs (power, cooling, networking) of $500 million to $1 billion

    Inference clusters are deployed by providers of consumer-facing AI services to execute trained models on user inputs. These clusters are typically smaller than training clusters (50-200 GPUs) but are deployed in much larger quantities (dozens to thousands globally). Inference clusters require:

    • 30-150 specialized servers
    • Optimized for throughput and latency rather than training capability
    • Lower overall infrastructure cost ($200-500 million per cluster)
    • Annual operational costs lower than training clusters

    By June 2030, global count of large AI clusters (training and inference combined) exceeded 12,000, with new clusters launching monthly.

    Infrastructure Supply Chain and Margins

    The server hardware suppliers (Dell, HPE, Supermicro, Lenovo) profit not from the GPUs or accelerators (supplied by NVIDIA, AMD) but from the system integration‚Äîdesigning specialized servers optimized for AI workloads, integrating GPUs/accelerators into the server architecture, designing thermal and power delivery systems, providing system-level optimization, and offering customization for specific customer requirements.

    Gross margins for AI-optimized servers are compressed relative to traditional server sales, averaging 16-18% for specialized AI server products. However, volume growth more than compensates for margin compression:

    • FY2025: Dell ISG gross margin approximately 15.2%, volume 1.6 million servers
    • FY2030: Dell ISG gross margin approximately 16.8%, volume 3.2 million servers

    Unit volume growth of 100% plus modest gross margin expansion resulted in gross profit growth of approximately 140% from FY2025 to FY2030 on the ISG business.

    SECTION 2: DELL'S FINANCIAL TRANSFORMATION

    Revenue Segmentation and Growth Dynamics

    Dell's consolidated financial performance shows the company in transformation from a diversified computing company toward an AI infrastructure specialist:

    Fiscal Year 2030 Revenue ($103.2 billion):

    • Infrastructure Solutions Group (ISG): $54.2 billion (52% of total)
    • AI server systems: $42.8 billion
    • Traditional enterprise servers: $8.1 billion

    • Enterprise storage: $3.3 billion

    • Client Solutions Group (CSG): $31.4 billion (30% of total)

    • Commercial PCs: $18.6 billion
    • Consumer PCs: $7.2 billion

    • Commercial displays and peripherals: $5.6 billion

    • EMC Data Storage: $12.1 billion (12% of total)

    • Enterprise storage systems

    • Cloud-native storage

    • Services and Software: $5.5 billion (5% of total)

    • Consulting, implementation, support
    • Cloud and managed services

    Growth Trajectory Analysis

    The five-year financial trajectory from FY2025 to FY2030 reveals a company fundamentally transforming its growth engines:

    FY2025 Baseline:
    - Total revenue: $92.2 billion
    - ISG revenue: $24.8 billion (26% of total)
    - Net income: $6.1 billion (6.6% net margin)

    FY2028 Midpoint:
    - Total revenue: $98.4 billion
    - ISG revenue: $38.1 billion (38% of total)
    - Net income: $7.9 billion (8.0% net margin)

    FY2030 Current:
    - Total revenue: $103.2 billion
    - ISG revenue: $54.2 billion (52% of total)
    - Net income: $8.7 billion (8.4% net margin)

    Compound Annual Growth Rates (2025-2030):
    - Total company revenue CAGR: 2.4%
    - ISG revenue CAGR: 16.9%
    - Net income CAGR: 7.4%

    The divergence between total company revenue growth (2.4%) and ISG revenue growth (16.9%) demonstrates the impact of declining PC business. The CSG PC business has contracted at approximately 6-8% compounded annually, partially offsetting ISG growth.

    Net Income and Margin Dynamics

    Net income grew 42.6% from FY2025 to FY2030 despite total revenue growth of only 12%, indicating significant margin improvement and operating leverage.

    This margin expansion resulted from:

    1. ISG margin improvement: ISG net margin (ISG revenue minus all allocated costs) has expanded from approximately 5.2% (FY2025) to 6.8% (FY2030) through manufacturing efficiency, supply chain optimization, and scale advantages.

    2. Fixed cost leverage: As ISG revenue has grown substantially, fixed costs (corporate overhead, R&D, sales infrastructure) have been distributed across a larger revenue base, improving overall company margins.

    3. Product mix: Higher-margin specialized AI servers represent a larger percentage of ISG revenue than traditional servers, improving blended ISG margins.

    4. Cost reduction: Dell has executed systematic cost reduction in legacy business (PC manufacturing, EMC operations) to mitigate margin pressure from volume declines.

    SECTION 3: THE PC BUSINESS DECLINE AND STRUCTURAL SHIFTS

    The Historical PC Dominance

    Historically, Dell's brand identity was centered on PC manufacturing. The Client Solutions Group (PCs) generated the majority of company revenue and was the most visible Dell product to consumers and businesses.

    By FY2025, following the pandemic-driven PC demand surge (2020-2021) and subsequent normalization, the CSG business represented approximately 49% of consolidated revenue ($45.6 billion) and was declining modestly (1-2% annually) due to:

    • Market maturation (most replacement PCs were 3-5 year old devices)
    • Shift to cloud-based computing reducing client-side computing requirements
    • Mobile device competition
    • Longer replacement cycles as PC performance plateaued

    The AI-Driven Structural Decline (2025-2030)

    Between 2025 and 2030, CSG decline accelerated dramatically as artificial intelligence systems began automating functions previously dependent on individual workstations:

    AI agents and cloud-based alternatives: As AI systems became capable of autonomous information summarization, writing assistance, coding support, and analytical work, the requirement for individuals to maintain high-performance local workstations declined.

    Knowledge workers could increasingly rely on cloud-based AI assistants for cognitive tasks that previously required local computing power. This reduced the economic justification for purchasing high-performance PCs.

    Enterprise consolidation: Large enterprises, already managing thousands of PCs, began consolidating to minimal-spec workstations for data entry and cloud connectivity, reducing PC costs per employee and limiting refresh cycle frequency.

    Shift to cloud applications: Migration from locally-installed enterprise applications to cloud-delivered software (Salesforce, Workday, etc.) reduced performance requirements for client devices.

    Mobile-first alternatives: For many job functions (sales, field service, logistics), mobile devices (tablets, smartphones) provided sufficient capability for job tasks, eliminating the need for PCs entirely.

    CSG Management and Margin Maintenance

    Despite volume declines of 31% (revenue decline from $45.6B to $31.4B), Dell management successfully maintained CSG net margins at approximately 18-22%, avoiding the catastrophic margin compression that would have resulted from volume reduction alone.

    This margin maintenance was achieved through:

    1. Product mix shift: Higher-margin commercial-grade PCs (sold to enterprises with volume discounts and support contracts) represented a larger percentage of mix than consumer PCs (sold through retail with lower margins).

    2. Premium segment focus: Dell shifted marketing emphasis toward premium-segment PCs (high-performance workstations for design/engineering, gaming-focused systems) where pricing power remained strong despite volume decline.

    3. Cost structure reduction: Dell rationalized manufacturing, reduced SKU complexity, and consolidated supplier relationships to reduce per-unit manufacturing costs despite lower volume.

    4. Services bundling: Sale of services, warranty, and support services alongside PC hardware improved gross margin percentage and customer lifetime value.

    By FY2030, CSG operations were optimized as a lower-growth, high-margin cash generation business serving enterprise customers with specific needs, rather than a mass-market consumer business.

    SECTION 4: COMPETITIVE POSITION IN AI SERVER MARKET

    Market Structure and Competitive Landscape

    The AI server infrastructure market has consolidated around four major suppliers and a fragmented tier of smaller players:

    Dell Technologies: Estimated 52% market share, approximately $54.2 billion in ISG revenue equivalent (derived from actual reported ISG revenue less traditional servers and storage). Dominant position in enterprise-grade AI servers for hyperscalers and large enterprises.

    Hewlett Packard Enterprise: Estimated 18% market share, approximately $19 billion in revenue equivalent from AI server operations. HPE maintains strong relationships with enterprise customers but has smaller presence with hyperscaler customers.

    Super Micro Computer: Estimated 15% market share, approximately $14 billion in revenue equivalent. Supermicro emphasizes customization and has particular strength with hyperscaler customers who build proprietary systems. Supermicro's strategy focuses on maximizing system customization and configuration flexibility.

    Lenovo: Estimated 10% market share, approximately $9 billion in revenue equivalent. Lenovo's position is growing, leveraging relationships in enterprise and Chinese market.

    Smaller competitors and fragmented suppliers: Approximately 5% market share collectively, including specialized vendors serving niche segments (GPU-less AI infrastructure, edge inference systems).

    Dell's Competitive Advantages

    Vertical Integration and Supply Chain Control: Dell owns manufacturing capacity and has direct relationships with component suppliers (motherboard vendors, power supply manufacturers, thermal solution providers). This enables Dell to:
    - Control component supply in constrained markets
    - Customize system designs rapidly for specific customer needs
    - Achieve lower per-unit manufacturing costs at scale
    - Reduce time-to-market for new system configurations

    Established Enterprise Relationships: Dell has decades-long relationships with enterprise IT organizations. These relationships provide:
    - Trust and confidence in system reliability and support
    - Established procurement and approval processes
    - Integration with existing Dell infrastructure (storage, networking, software)
    - High switching costs (enterprises reluctant to introduce new suppliers)

    Integrated Portfolio: Dell's ability to combine servers, storage, networking, and software into complete solutions provides competitive advantage. Hyperscalers building custom systems may not value this integration, but enterprises do.

    Manufacturing Scale and Efficiency: Dell's high-volume server manufacturing gives the company cost advantages in areas like:
    - Power supply efficiency and cost
    - Thermal solution design and manufacturing
    - Customization at scale (ability to offer numerous configurations without SKU proliferation)

    Competitive Vulnerabilities

    Hyperscaler Custom Chips Strategy: The most significant long-term competitive risk is that major hyperscalers (Google, Amazon, Meta) accelerate development of custom silicon optimized for their specific AI workloads. If hyperscalers achieve sufficient custom chip development, they could internalize server design and manufacturing, reducing purchases from suppliers like Dell.

    This risk manifests as follows:
    - Google has developed TPU (Tensor Processing Unit) accelerators optimized for Google workloads. Google may increasingly design custom servers around these TPUs rather than purchasing off-the-shelf systems.
    - Amazon has invested in custom chips (Trainium, Inferentia) for ML workloads. Amazon may reduce purchase volumes from Dell as Amazon increases internal manufacturing.
    - Meta has explored custom accelerators and may reduce reliance on NVIDIA GPUs and Dell servers if proprietary systems prove competitive.

    This dynamic has occurred historically in computing infrastructure (Microsoft, Apple, and other large technology companies invested in custom chip design). If hyperscalers successfully execute custom chip strategies, Dell's revenue from hyperscaler customers could decline 20-40% over 5-10 years.

    Margin Compression Dynamics: Server competition is characterized by commoditization and margin compression. As more competitors enter the AI server market and differentiation becomes harder (all vendors use NVIDIA GPUs, PCIe interfaces, standard power supplies), competitive pricing pressure increases.

    Dell's ISG gross margins of 16.8% are already compressed relative to some other infrastructure businesses. Further margin compression to 14-15% would significantly impact profitability despite continued volume growth.

    Taiwan Semiconductor Supply Concentration: A substantial portion of AI server components (memory, storage, networking chips) are manufactured in Taiwan. Geopolitical risk to Taiwan (US-China tensions, potential military conflict) creates vulnerability to supply disruption.

    If Taiwan semiconductor supply were disrupted, Dell's ability to manufacture servers would be severely impaired. This represents a structural risk beyond Dell's control.

    SECTION 5: VALUATION ANALYSIS AND SCENARIOS

    Current Market Valuation (June 2030)

    Stock Price: $142.30 per share
    Market Capitalization: $211.2 billion
    Forward P/E Ratio: 18.4x
    Price-to-Sales Ratio: 2.0x
    Enterprise Value-to-EBITDA: 8.1x
    Free Cash Flow Yield: 4.2%

    These valuation multiples reflect investor pricing of Dell as a growth company within the AI infrastructure space, but not an exceptionally expensive valuation.

    Comparative context:
    - Semiconductor equipment manufacturers (ASML, LRCX): 28-35x forward P/E
    - Cloud infrastructure providers (Cloudflare, Fastly): 18-24x forward P/E
    - Traditional hardware manufacturers (HPE, Supermicro): 12-16x forward P/E
    - Mature technology companies (IBM, HP): 8-12x forward P/E

    Dell's 18.4x P/E places it in the upper-middle range‚Äîpricing in growth but not premium valuations.

    Bull Case Scenario (2030-2035)

    Assumptions:
    - AI infrastructure demand continues to accelerate; AI clusters grow from 12,000 (2030) to 35,000+ by 2035
    - AI server unit shipments grow 18-22% compounded annually
    - ISG revenue reaches $90+ billion by 2035
    - ISG gross margins expand to 19-20% through manufacturing scale and operational leverage
    - Company-wide net margins expand to 10-11%
    - PC business stabilizes at approximately $25-28 billion (minimal further decline)

    Financial Projections:
    - FY2035 consolidated revenue: $140-145 billion
    - FY2035 net income: $14-16 billion
    - FY2035 P/E multiple: 20-22x (slight multiple expansion on proven growth)

    Valuation Implications:
    - FY2035 stock price: $280-350 per share
    - Compound annual return (2030-2035): 18-25% annually

    This bull case assumes continued exceptional demand for AI infrastructure, successful execution by Dell, and margin improvement. While possible, it requires several favorable assumptions.

    Base Case Scenario (2030-2035)

    Assumptions:
    - AI infrastructure demand grows 12-15% compounded annually (moderation from recent growth rates)
    - AI server shipments grow 13-16% annually
    - ISG revenue reaches $75-80 billion by 2035
    - ISG gross margins stable at 16.5-17.5%
    - Company-wide net margins remain in 8-9% range
    - PC business declines modestly to $28-31 billion

    Financial Projections:
    - FY2035 consolidated revenue: $128-135 billion
    - FY2035 net income: $10.5-12 billion
    - FY2035 P/E multiple: 18-19x (stable to slight contraction)

    Valuation Implications:
    - FY2035 stock price: $188-228 per share
    - Compound annual return (2030-2035): 5-10% annually

    This base case assumes continued strong AI infrastructure demand but moderation from recent exceptional growth rates. It also assumes margins remain stable despite competitive pressures.

    Bear Case Scenario (2030-2035)

    Assumptions:
    - Hyperscalers accelerate custom chip strategies; reduce Dell server purchases 25-35%
    - AI infrastructure demand growth moderates to 6-8% annually
    - Competitive pressure intensifies; margins compress to 14-15%
    - ISG revenue reaches $65-70 billion by 2035 (growth from volume increases offset by custom chip impact)
    - Company-wide net margins compress to 7-8%
    - PC business declines further to $24-26 billion

    Financial Projections:
    - FY2035 consolidated revenue: $118-125 billion
    - FY2035 net income: $8-10 billion (below current levels)
    - FY2035 P/E multiple: 15-16x (contraction on slower growth and margin pressure)

    Valuation Implications:
    - FY2035 stock price: $120-160 per share
    - Compound annual return (2030-2035): -3% to +2% annually (potential downside)

    This bear case reflects risks from hyperscaler custom chips, competitive pressures, and margin compression.

    Fair Value Assessment

    Given the three scenarios and their probabilities:
    - Bull case (25% probability): $280-350
    - Base case (50% probability): $188-228
    - Bear case (25% probability): $120-160

    Weighted expected value by 2035: Approximately $195-220 per share

    This implies current price of $142.30 represents approximately 20-25% upside to fair value over five years, or approximately 4-5% compound annual return.

    Fair valuation range at current date (June 2030): $140-160 per share

    Current stock price of $142.30 represents fair value with balanced risk/reward, slightly favoring bull case assumptions.

    SECTION 6: KEY RISKS AND UNCERTAINTIES

    Supply Chain and Geopolitical Risks

    Taiwan semiconductor dependency creates existential vulnerability. Approximately 35-40% of critical server components (memory, storage controllers, high-speed networking chips) are manufactured in Taiwan.

    A supply disruption from Taiwan (from geopolitical conflict, natural disaster, or other causes) would severely impair Dell's manufacturing capability, potentially taking weeks to months to recover.

    Custom Chip Development Trajectory

    Hyperscaler custom chip development timelines are uncertain. Current status:
    - Google TPU development ongoing; scale and performance trajectory unclear
    - Amazon Trainium/Inferentia in limited deployment
    - Meta custom chip development rumored but not officially confirmed

    If hyperscalers achieve 50%+ of infrastructure via custom systems by 2035, Dell's ISG revenue could decline 20-30%.

    Artificial Intelligence Adoption Rate Uncertainty

    Base case assumes AI infrastructure demand grows 12-15% annually through 2035. This is predicated on assumptions about AI adoption rate, enterprise spending, and technology development.

    If AI adoption stalls (regulatory restrictions, technical plateaus, lower-than-expected ROI) or if alternative approaches to AI become prevalent (edge AI, federated learning, quantum computing), infrastructure demand could be substantially lower.

    Competitive Intensity

    New competitors continue entering the AI server market (Inspur, H3C, others). As AI server market matures, competition intensifies and margins compress. Dell's ability to maintain 16-18% ISG margins is not guaranteed.

    THE BULL CASE ALTERNATIVE: Hyperscaler Integration and Margin Expansion

    If Dell successfully positions itself as essential systems integrator (reducing hyperscaler custom chip ROI calculus), AI infrastructure demand accelerates beyond projections, and manufacturing scale improves gross margins to 19-20%, then the company could achieve $90B+ ISG revenue by 2035 with 18-22% growth rates. Combined with stabilized PC business and margin expansion to 10-11% company-wide, stock could reach $280-350 by 2035 supporting 18-25% CAGR returns. This "picks and shovels" thesis remains valid if hyperscaler custom chip adoption rates disappoint.

    CONCLUSION: A JUSTIFIED BUT FAIR VALUATION

    By June 2030, Dell Technologies had successfully executed a strategic pivot toward AI infrastructure specialization. The company's financial results, market position, and growth trajectory vindicated the "picks and shovels" investment thesis.

    However, the valuation at $142.30 per share reflects these achievements and growth prospects, leaving limited room for additional valuation expansion unless Dell exceeds expectations.

    The stock is appropriate for investors seeking:
    - AI infrastructure exposure through hardware/manufacturing channel
    - Reasonable valuation relative to growth rate
    - Dividend potential (modest, at approximately 1.2% yield)
    - Lower volatility than direct semiconductor or AI software plays
    - Bull case upside if hyperscaler custom chip economics disappoint

    The stock should be avoided by investors expecting exceptional returns (unless bearing bull case conviction), or those uncomfortable with hyperscaler custom chip risks.

    At current levels, Dell offers fair risk/reward with 5-10% annual return potential in base case scenarios, with 18-25% upside if hyperscaler custom chip adoption rates slow.

    WHAT YOU SHOULD DO NOW

    The investment landscape described in this memo creates clear winners and losers. Here's how to position yourself:

    Immediate portfolio review: Evaluate your holdings against the bear case scenarios described above. Companies with high labor costs, low AI adoption, and reactive management are value traps, not value investments.

    AI transformation screening: For every company in your portfolio, answer three questions: (1) Has the CEO publicly committed to AI transformation with specific capital allocation? (2) Is the company investing 3%+ of revenue in AI capabilities? (3) Does the company have a credible plan for workforce transition? If the answer to all three is no, consider your exit timeline.

    Sector allocation: Overweight sectors where AI creates new value (healthcare AI, AI infrastructure, AI-enabled services). Underweight sectors where AI primarily destroys existing value without creating new revenue streams.

    Geographic diversification: Countries with proactive AI policies, strong education systems, and flexible labor markets will outperform. Evaluate your geographic exposure accordingly.

    Due diligence upgrade: Add AI readiness assessment to your standard due diligence process. Ask management teams specifically about their AI strategy, timeline, and capital commitment. Vague answers are a red flag.
