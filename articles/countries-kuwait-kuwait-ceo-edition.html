Regional Leadership and Benchmarking'] Kuwait's Vision 2035 represents an ambitious strategic development plan adopted in 2017 with the overarching goal of transforming the nation into a globally competitive, knowledge-based economy by 2035. The vision was born from recognition that Kuwait's economy, historically reliant on oil exports, requires fundamental structural transformation to ensure long-term prosperity and global competitiveness. This strategic initiative extends far beyond simple economic diversification; it represents a comprehensive reimagining of Kuwait's role in the global economy and a commitment to innovation, sustainability, and human capital development. At the heart of Vision 2035 lies recognition that artificial intelligence and digital transformation are not optional enhancements but fundamental pillars of modern economic competitiveness. Kuwait's strategic planners identified that nations failing to invest in AI capabilities risk obsolescence in the rapidly evolving global technological landscape. Consequently, the vision explicitly positions AI as a transformative technology that will permeate government operations, private sector activities, healthcare delivery, education, and infrastructure management. This comprehensive approach distinguishes Kuwait's AI strategy from narrower, sector-specific initiatives in other nations. The National AI Strategy, formulated as part of Vision 2035's implementation roadmap, establishes concrete objectives for Kuwait to become a regional leader in AI innovation by 2028. This intermediate milestone serves as a critical checkpoint for measuring progress toward the broader 2035 vision. The strategy identifies key sectors for AI application including government services, healthcare, energy, education, and financial services. By 2028, Kuwait aspires to have AI deeply embedded in these sectors, with measurable improvements in service delivery, operational efficiency, and innovation metrics. Government entities are being encouraged to adopt AI-driven decision-making systems, implement predictive analytics for resource allocation, and develop intelligent systems for citizen service delivery. Central to Kuwait's AI development strategy is the commitment to developing local large language models and Arabic-language AI capabilities. International LLMs, while powerful, often reflect linguistic and cultural assumptions embedded in their training data, which may not align with Arabic cultural contexts or effectively serve Arabic-speaking populations. By developing indigenous Arabic-language AI models, Kuwait ensures that technological advancement serves local needs while creating opportunities for regional expertise and intellectual property development. This initiative also positions Kuwait as a potential hub for Arabic AI research and development, with potential to serve markets across the Arabic-speaking world, from North Africa to the Levant. The government has taken significant steps to modernize its technological infrastructure in support of these ambitions. The Central Agency for Information Technology (CAIT) leads digitalization efforts, with targets to digitalize more than 90% of government services within five years. This massive infrastructure modernization project, valued at approximately $193 billion in active projects, encompasses smart cities, digital identity systems, biometric technologies, and cloud computing infrastructure. The scale of this investment demonstrates genuine government commitment to Vision 2035 implementation rather than aspirational rhetoric. A landmark partnership between CAIT, CITRA (Communications and Information Technology Regulatory Authority), and Microsoft, formalized in March 2025, exemplifies Kuwait's strategy of international collaboration. The partnership includes several concrete commitments: establishment of an AI data center within Kuwait, development of integrated government AI systems, creation of a center for digital economy promotion, and establishment of a center of excellence for cloud auditing. This partnership leverages Microsoft's technological expertise while ensuring that AI development occurs within Kuwait's borders and under Kuwaiti governance, addressing data sovereignty concerns and building local technical capacity. Kuwait's 5G infrastructure, already among the world's most advanced, provides crucial foundation for AI and digital services deployment. As the first regional nation to implement 5G, Kuwait has invested heavily in modern telecommunications infrastructure. Chinese company Huawei's $1.7 billion investment license to develop Kuwait's ICT sector further enhances technological capacity. This convergence of 5G infrastructure, cloud computing resources, and AI development capacity creates an increasingly compelling environment for technology-focused investment and innovation. The regulatory environment supporting these initiatives continues to evolve. CITRA, as the primary telecommunications and technology regulator, is developing policies and regulatory frameworks to enable modern technologies while protecting cybersecurity, public interest, and human values. This balanced approach recognizes that unregulated technological advancement risks creating vulnerabilities and social harms, while over-regulation can stifle innovation. The challenge of regulatory design—fostering innovation while ensuring responsible deployment—will significantly influence Vision 2035's success. Kuwait's aspirations to become a regional AI leader reflect both economic logic and strategic positioning. The UAE, Saudi Arabia, and other regional competitors have similarly invested heavily in AI capabilities, creating a competitive dynamic that encourages acceleration of Kuwait's efforts. Yet Kuwait's specific advantages—high per-capita income ($31,944 in 2023), existing government digital infrastructure investments, and substantial financial resources—position it competitively if execution matches ambition. Vision 2035's success will depend critically on whether Kuwait can sustain investment momentum, retain local talent, and effectively implement the regulatory framework while competing for regional AI talent and investment.
