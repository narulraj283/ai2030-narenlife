    ENTITY: BERKSHIRE HATHAWAY

    A Macro Intelligence Memo | June 2030 | Investor Edition

    SUMMARY: THE BEAR CASE vs. THE BULL CASE

    BEAR CASE: Regulatory crackdown on utilities reduces BHE returns to 7-8%. Insurance mega-loss compresses ROE. Manufacturing faces margin pressure from ongoing AI labor substitution. Berkshire ROE declines to 10-11%. Stock declines to $110-130/share by 2035.

    BULL CASE: BHE accelerates electrification adoption (8%+ annual growth). Insurance underwriting cycle improves. Manufacturing efficiency gains expand margins. Berkshire ROE expands to 14-15%. Stock rallies to $225-250/share by 2035 (+52% to +70% upside).

    REALISTIC CASE: BHE steady-state 5-6% growth from capex deployment. Insurance normal underwriting cycle. Manufacturing stable margins. ROE: 12.8% maintained through 2035. Stock reaches $185-210/share (25-45% upside) as "AI-safe" positioning attracts institutional capital.

    EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

    Berkshire Hathaway has emerged as the defining winner of the 2025-2030 period not through bold AI bets or transformative acquisitions, but through methodical execution in boring, capital-intensive businesses that proved immune to algorithmic disruption. While the Magnificent Seven compressed 65-75%, GPU manufacturers imploded, and enterprise software struggled with margin compression, Berkshire delivered 11.8% annualized total returns through a portfolio anchored in insurance, utilities, railroads, and manufacturing. The thesis is straightforward: in an era where software valuations collapsed under the weight of AI commoditization and labor cost devastation, Berkshire's dominance in physical infrastructure proved durable. This memo outlines the investment case through 2030 and identifies sustained tailwinds through 2035.

    Investment Rating: BUY | Price Target: $144/share (Class B equivalent) | 12-month return potential: 12-14%

    I. THE DISRUPTION THESIS: WHY BERKSHIRE WON

    Between 2025 and 2028, the technology and software sectors experienced synchronized valuation compression as three converging forces shifted capital allocation:

    1. GPU Commodity Crisis (2025-2027): NVIDIA, AMD, and Broadcom collectively lost 68% of market value as AI training capacity exceeded demand by 40-50%, and inference workloads automated toward cheaper, custom silicon. The peak-GPU cycle extracted $2.4 trillion in value destruction across hardware, cloud services, and downstream enterprise spending.

    2. Enterprise Software Labor Automation (2026-2028): Payroll processing, customer service, knowledge work, and business process automation displaced 8.2 million white-collar jobs in developed markets, collapsing SaaS pricing power and recurring revenue multiples. Enterprise software traded at an average 18x forward revenue by mid-2028 (down from 28x in 2024).

    3. Venture Capital Collapse (2026-2030): AI startup formation collapsed 62% by 2027 as venture capital returns proved negative, and founder liquidation preferences exceeded realistic exit multiples. The median Series B valuation declined 48% between 2025 and 2029.

    Berkshire, conversely, had minimal exposure to any of these dynamics. The portfolio was deliberately constructed to avoid disruption vector concentration.

    Key Portfolio Composition (June 2030):
    - Insurance operations: 28% of intrinsic value (~$420B)
    - Utilities and energy: 19% (~$285B)
    - Railroads (BNSF): 11% (~$165B)
    - Brownfield manufacturing (Marmon, Precision Castparts): 15% (~$225B)
    - Bank equities and secured lending: 12% (~$180B)
    - Cash and equivalents: 8% (~$120B)
    - Other (Berkshire Hathaway Energy, See's Candies, etc.): 7% (~$105B)

    Total estimated intrinsic value: $1.48 trillion | Class A equivalent: $2,220/share | Class B: $148/share

    II. INSURANCE: THE DURABLE MOAT

    Berkshire's insurance operations‚Äîcomprising GEICO, Berkshire Hathaway Reinsurance (BH Re), and specialty underwriting‚Äîgenerated $68.4 billion in premiums in 2030, with a consolidated underwriting profit of $8.2 billion (12% underwriting margin). This represents the highest annual underwriting margin in the company's history.

    Why Insurance Proved Durable During AI Disruption:

    Unlike claims processing automation (which benefited insurers in 2025-2027), the core insurance economics remained unchanged: the company collects premiums from millions of customers and pays claims with a lag, creating a float-based investment vehicle. GEICO's 24-month customer retention rate reached 89% by 2030, up from 82% in 2025, as the brand built moat through habit, price competitiveness, and brand loyalty.

    The insurance float‚Äîcapital available for investment until claims are paid‚Äîgrew to $186 billion by June 2030, up from $168 billion in 2025. This float funds Berkshire's other investments and provides the capital base for opportunistic acquisitions during market stress.

    Insurance Profitability Drivers:

    1. Premium Growth: Berkshire's insurance portfolio grew 5.8% annually between 2025 and 2030, outpacing industry growth of 3.2%. GEICO retained pricing power through brand strength, and BH Re benefited from capacity shortage as competitors retreated from reinsurance.

    2. Investment Income: With $186B in insurance float earning an average yield of 5.4% (up from 2.1% in 2025 as interest rates normalized), investment income from insurance reserves generated $10.0 billion annually by 2030.

    3. Claims Management: AI-assisted claims triage and fraud detection deployed across GEICO and BH Re reduced loss ratios by 2.4 percentage points between 2025 and 2030. Predictive modeling identified high-risk claims profiles, reducing claims severity.

    The insurance business is not exciting. It is also not disrupted by AI. It is the foundation of Berkshire's durability.

    III. UTILITIES: STRUCTURAL GROWTH FROM ELECTRIFICATION

    Berkshire Hathaway Energy (BHE), the company's 90%-owned utility holding company, grew operating earnings to $18.3 billion in 2030, up 38% from $13.2 billion in 2025. This represents the fastest growth period in the company's history.

    Drivers of Utility Earnings Growth:

    1. Electrification Demand (2025-2030): Transport electrification, building heating conversion, and industrial process electrification drove incremental electricity demand of 7.2% annually across BHE's 16-state service territory. This incremental demand required $34 billion in capital investment between 2025 and 2030.

    2. Regulated Rate Base Expansion: BHE's regulated rate base grew from $184 billion (2025) to $264 billion (2030), a 43% increase. Each dollar of rate base generates stable 9.5-10.2% returns under regulatory frameworks in California, Nevada, and Iowa.

    3. Renewable Energy Transition: BHE invested $28 billion in solar, wind, and battery storage between 2025 and 2030, doubling renewable capacity to 41% of generation. Renewable assets operate with 3-5% annual cost declines and provide stable 8-9% returns.

    4. AI-Driven Grid Optimization: Berkshire deployed machine learning algorithms across BHE's transmission network to optimize load balancing, reduce transmission losses, and forecast demand with 94% accuracy (vs. 78% in 2025). These efficiency gains generated $1.2 billion in annual cost savings.

    Utilities are defensive, capital-intensive, and structurally positioned for 25+ years of steady earnings growth as electrification proceeds. Valuations reflect this durability: BHE trades at 12.8x forward earnings with a 3.8% dividend yield.

    IV. MANUFACTURING AND CAPITAL ALLOCATION

    Berkshire's Brownfield manufacturing holdings‚ÄîMarmon Industries, Precision Castparts Corp, and assorted industrial businesses‚Äîreported combined operating earnings of $12.4 billion in 2030. These businesses benefited from structural supply chain reorientation.

    AI Impact on Manufacturing (2025-2030):

    Between 2025 and 2028, manufacturers deployed industrial AI across production facilities, achieving:
    - 12-18% reduction in defect rates
    - 8-14% improvement in asset utilization (uptime and throughput)
    - 22-31% reduction in unplanned maintenance through predictive maintenance algorithms

    Berkshire's manufacturing operations deployed AI systems at 34 major facilities, generating incremental EBITDA of $2.1 billion between 2025 and 2030. These AI investments required $340 million in capex but delivered 6.2-year payback periods.

    Capital Allocation Philosophy:

    Berkshire maintained its core capital allocation discipline: reinvest earnings into businesses trading below intrinsic value, repurchase Berkshire shares below intrinsic value, and accumulate float/cash for opportunistic deployment during crises.

    In 2027, during the acute venture capital collapse and technology sector valuation crash, Berkshire deployed $24 billion acquiring distressed equity positions, long-term credit positions, and subsidiary investments. These 2027 acquisitions had generated 18.4% annualized returns through June 2030.

    Share repurchases totaled $47 billion between 2025 and 2030, reducing share count by 8.2% and offsetting dilution from subsidiary earnings and management incentive systems.

    VI. RISKS AND VALUATION

    Key Risks:

    1. Regulatory Risk: BHE's earnings depend on rate-setting bodies maintaining 9-10% regulatory returns. Aggressive rate suppression could reduce utility earnings 15-25%.

    2. Insurance Underwriting Cycle: Large catastrophic losses (2-in-10-year event) could generate underwriting losses of $4-8 billion, compressing ROE temporarily.

    3. Secular Margin Compression: Manufacturing and business services face structural margin compression if AI-driven commoditization accelerates. Marmon's combined 8.2% operating margin could compress to 6-7% if labor cost arbitrage disappears.

    4. Valuation: At 1.30x book value, Berkshire trades near historical averages but offers modest upside if valuations don't expand. Near-zero multiple expansion scenario implies 4-6% annual returns.

    Valuation Analysis:

    Using normalized ROE of 12.8% and perpetual growth of 4%, intrinsic value per share is $148 (Class B equivalent). Current trading range of $138-142 implies 3-7% upside to fair value. The 2-3 year price target of $165-174 assumes modest multiple expansion to 1.38-1.42x book as Berkshire's defensive positioning increases institutional allocation.

    VII. COMPETITIVE POSITIONING AND EARNINGS DURABILITY

    Berkshire's competitive position in June 2030 reflected decades of accumulated economic moats that proved resilient against AI disruption:

    Insurance Moat:
    - GEICO: 24-month retention rate of 89% (vs. industry 72%) - customer switching costs and brand loyalty
    - Underwriting expertise: 70+ years of claims data enabling superior risk pricing
    - Float arbitrage: $186B float generating positive returns even at low underwriting margins

    Utility Moat:
    - Regulated assets: 41GW of generation capacity with contractual rate guarantees
    - Capital intensity barrier: $264B rate base requiring capital deployment; competitors cannot easily replicate
    - Geographic advantage: BHE dominates 16-state West and Midwest region with no practical competitors

    Manufacturing Moat:
    - Operational excellence: Marmon's 34-site manufacturing network achieves efficiency through scale
    - Proprietary processes: Precision Castparts' aerospace manufacturing processes not easily commoditized
    - Customer relationships: Century-long client relationships with Boeing, aerospace industry (not price-based churn)

    Berkshire's moats proved that in AI disruption era, sustainable competitive advantages depend on network effects, switching costs, regulatory protection, or operational complexity‚Äînot on technological prowess.

    VIII. ORGANIZATIONAL MANAGEMENT AND SUCCESSION

    Warren Buffett Leadership Through 2030

    By June 2030, Warren Buffett (age 100) continued as CEO while incrementally delegating operational responsibilities to successor management team. The company faced market concerns about leadership transition and succession risk.

    Succession Planning (Implemented 2025-2030):
    - Greg Abel (Vice Chairman) elevated to President in 2029; positioned as eventual CEO successor
    - Ajit Jain (Vice Chairman Insurance) managing insurance operations; 68 years old
    - Todd Combs and Ted Weschler (Investment managers) managing $300B+ portfolio together

    Market pricing incorporated modest succession risk discount: Berkshire traded at 1.30x book (vs. 1.35-1.40x during peak Buffett era) reflecting investor concerns about post-Buffett strategy.

    Post-Succession Outlook

    Upon Buffett's transition, key question: Will Greg Abel continue Buffett's disciplined capital allocation (underweight tech, overweight utilities/insurance) or pursue more aggressive growth/acquisition strategy?

    Current consensus: Abel likely to maintain core Buffett philosophy (disciplined, long-term focused) but with slightly more aggressive willingness to deploy capital for large acquisitions. This is viewed modestly positively by investors (+2-3% multiple expansion potential).

    IX. AI INTEGRATION AND OPERATIONAL EFFICIENCY GAINS

    Despite being "AI-resistant," Berkshire achieved significant operational efficiency through thoughtful AI deployment:

    Deployed AI Systems (By 2030)

    • Insurance claims triage and fraud detection: Generated $2.1B annual benefits
    • Manufacturing predictive maintenance: Generated $1.1B annual benefits
    • Utility grid optimization and load forecasting: Generated $1.2B annual benefits
    • Portfolio analytics and risk monitoring: Generated $650M in avoided losses

    Total AI-driven operational benefits: $5.05 billion annually by 2030

    These benefits were incremental efficiency improvements, not business transformations. Berkshire's philosophy: use AI to optimize existing operations, not to transform business models.

    X. DOWNSIDE SCENARIOS AND STRESS TESTING

    Scenario 1: Regulatory Crackdown on Utilities (25% probability)

    If aggressive regulators cap utility returns at 7-8% (vs. current 9-10%), BHE earnings could decline $2-3B annually. This would reduce Berkshire earnings 3-4%, implying stock price decline of 10-12%.

    Mitigation: BHE's regulatory profile is strong in conservative states; Democratic/Republican balance limits extreme outcomes

    Scenario 2: Insurance Mega-Loss (12% probability)

    1-in-50-year catastrophe (exceeding $20B in losses) could generate massive underwriting losses. Berkshire could absorb losses given float depth, but ROE would temporarily decline significantly.

    Mitigation: BHE's stable utility earnings would offset insurance losses; diversification protects overall returns

    Scenario 3: Recession and Unemployment Spike (18% probability)

    Economic contraction accelerating beyond current expectations (2030 GDP -4 to -5%) would reduce insurance premiums, auto lending defaults, and manufacturing demand. Berkshire positioned defensively (insurance covers unemployment, utilities are recession-resistant) but would see earnings decline 8-12%.

    Mitigation: Berkshire's balance sheet strength enables counter-cyclical deployment
