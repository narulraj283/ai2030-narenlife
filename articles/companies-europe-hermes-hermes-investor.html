    SECTION 4: SCARCITY AS ECONOMIC MOAT

    The Scarcity Business Model

    Herm√®s's fundamental business model contradicted traditional luxury strategy: rather than maximizing production and sales volume, Herm√®s deliberately limited supply to create scarcity:

    2. Waiting Lists:
    - Birkin bags: 2-4 year waiting lists (customers wait years to purchase)

    - Some exotic leather items: 3-5 year waiting lists
    - This creates psychological demand amplification‚Äîcustomers want products they must wait for

    3. Pricing Power Through Scarcity:
    - Regular 5-7% annual price increases (vs. 2-3% industry average)
    - Customers accept price increases because alternatives are waiting lists
    - Scarcity eliminates price competition‚Äînot competing on price, competing on access

    4. Brand Exclusivity:
    - Limited boutique distribution (not in malls, not in department stores)
    - Premium boutique locations (Monaco, Geneva, luxury districts)
    - Visual differentiation from mass luxury (no discount outlets, no clearance sales)

    Economics of Scarcity

    Traditional Luxury Logic (LVMH):
    - Maximize production ‚Üí maximize unit sales ‚Üí maximize revenue
    - Volume strategy creates scale economies
    - Risk: Brand dilution, competitive pressure, margin compression

    Herm√®s Scarcity Logic:
    - Limit production ‚Üí create scarcity ‚Üí increase pricing power
    - Scarcity strategy creates brand magic
    - Result: Higher margins despite lower volume

    Financial Impact:

    - Volume strategy at ‚Ç¨40B revenue, 20% margins = ‚Ç¨8B operating profit, with margin pressure and competitive intensity

    Scarcity strategy was more profitable per unit of invested capital and created brand magic. This explained why family-controlled Herm√®s pursued scarcity strategy despite lower absolute revenue.

    Sustainability of Scarcity Model

    Critics questioned whether scarcity was sustainable:
